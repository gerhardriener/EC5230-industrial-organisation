Part I. Market Structures

Perfect Competition Benchmark

Under perfect competition:

  • Price equals marginal cost: \(P = MC\)
  • Markets are allocatively efficient
  • Zero economic profit in long run
  • Homogeneous products
  • Many buyers and sellers

Imperfect Competition: Strategic Behavior

Under imperfect competition:

  • Price exceeds marginal cost: \(P > MC\)
  • Markets generate welfare losses
  • Strategic firm behavior matters
  • Product differentiation possible
  • Few firms with market power

Market Structures

Market Structure Typology

Three key competitive regimes:

Structure # Firms Price Setting Output
Perfect Competition Many Price-taker Competitive
Monopoly 1 Demand curve Lower
Cournot Oligopoly Few Strategic Intermediate

Cournot Quantity Competition: Model

Firm \(i\)’s maximization problem:

\[\max_{q_i} \left[q_i \left(P(q_i + q_{-i}) - c(q_i)\right)\right]\]

Variables: - \(q_i\): firm \(i\)’s quantity (choice variable) - \(q_{-i}\): rivals’ total quantity (parameter from firm \(i\)’s perspective) - \(P(\cdot)\): inverse demand function (parameter) - \(c(\cdot)\): cost function (parameter)

Cournot Pricing Formula

Equilibrium condition:

\[\frac{P - c'(q_i)}{P} = \frac{\alpha_i}{\eta}\]

Where: - \(\alpha_i = \frac{q_i}{Q}\) is firm \(i\)’s market share - \(\eta = -\frac{dQ/Q}{dP/P}\) is the price elasticity of demand - LHS is the Lerner index (mark-up rate)

Cournot Pricing Formula: Interpretation

Key Insight

The mark-up is directly proportional to market share and inversely related to demand elasticity.

  • Larger market share → higher mark-up
  • More elastic demand → lower mark-up

Welfare Analysis

Welfare Measures in Equilibrium

Three welfare concepts:

  • Consumer Surplus (CS): \(u(Q) - P(Q)Q\)
  • Producer Surplus (PS): \(P(Q)Q - C(Q)\)
  • Welfare Loss (WL): Deadweight loss from inefficiency

All measured relative to competitive benchmark.

Welfare Comparison Across Market Structures

Market Structure Consumer Surplus Producer Surplus Welfare Loss
Perfect Competition Max 0 0
Monopoly Low Max High
Cournot Oligopoly Intermediate Intermediate Intermediate

Cournot Efficiency: Interpretation

Key Finding

Cournot oligopoly equilibrium is constrained efficient: it maximizes a weighted combination of consumer and producer surplus—the outcome reflects strategic interdependence without coordination.

Implication: Oligopoly output lies strictly between monopoly and perfect competition.

Three-Firm Market: Setup

Parameters:

  • Demand: \(P = 20 - Q\)
  • Marginal costs: \(c_1 = 0\), \(c_2 = 5\), \(c_3 = 7\)

Three-Firm Market: Perfect Competition vs. Monopoly

Perfect Competition (firm 1 at \(p = MC = 0\)):

\[\text{CS} = 200, \quad \text{PS} = 0, \quad \text{WL} = 0\]

Monopoly (firm 1 alone):

\[\text{CS} = 50, \quad \text{PS} = 100, \quad \text{WL} = 50\]

Three-Firm Market: Cournot Triopoly

Cournot Triopoly:

\[\text{CS} = 72, \quad \text{PS} < 96, \quad \text{WL} > 32\]

Result: Oligopoly equilibrium output lies strictly between monopoly and perfect competition; price and profits are correspondingly intermediate.

Cournot Welfare Maximand

Equilibrium maximizes a weighted combination:

\[ \max_Q \left\{ (n-1)[u(Q) - P(Q)Q] + n[P(Q)Q - nc(Q/n)] \right\} \]

Components: - \((n-1)[u(Q) - P(Q)Q]\): weight on consumer surplus - \(n[P(Q)Q - nc(Q/n)]\): weight on producer surplus - \(n\) identical firms, \(u'(Q) = P(Q)\)

Welfare Weights: Perfect Competition

As \(n \to \infty\):

\[\text{Weight on CS} = (n-1) \to \infty\] \[\text{Weight on PS} = n \to \infty\]

Perfect Competition: Interpretation

Result: The welfare function places infinite weight on consumer surplus relative to individual firm profits.

Equilibrium: Maximizes CS subject to firms earning zero economic profit.

Welfare Weights: Monopoly

When \(n = 1\):

\[\text{Weight on CS} = (n-1) = 0\] \[\text{Weight on PS} = n = 1\]

Monopoly: Interpretation

Result: The monopolist ignores consumer surplus entirely and maximizes its own profit alone.

Equilibrium: Unconstrained monopoly outcome.

Welfare Weights: Cournot Oligopoly

When \(1 < n < \infty\):

\[\text{Weight on CS} = (n-1) \in (0, \infty)\] \[\text{Weight on PS} = n \in (1, \infty)\]

Cournot Oligopoly: Interpretation

Result: Oligopoly equilibrium balances consumer and producer surplus with intermediate weights.

The outcome reflects tension between: - Individual firm profit incentives (weight \(n\)) - Aggregate demand response to total output (weight \(n-1\))

Welfare Weights: Comparative Statics

Convergence to Competition

As \(n\) increases from 1 to \(\infty\), the weight on consumer surplus grows relative to producer surplus, driving equilibrium output toward the competitive level and price toward marginal cost.

Differentiated Products

Cournot Model with Differentiated Products

Inverse demand functions:

\[P_1 = a - bq_1 - dq_2\] \[P_2 = a - dq_1 - bq_2\]

Parameters: - \(b > |d|\) (own effect > cross effect) - \(d \in (-b, b)\) measures product differentiation

Product Differentiation Parameter: Interpretation

Interpretation of \(d\):

  • \(d < 0\): Goods are complements
  • \(d = 0\): Goods are independent
  • \(d > 0\): Goods are substitutes

Cournot Best Response with Substitutes

For substitute goods (\(d > 0\)):

\[BR_i(q_j) = \frac{a - dq_j - c_i}{2b}\]

Property: Downward-sloping best response.

Strategic Substitutes: Interpretation

Quantities are strategic substitutes:

If competitor increases output, my optimal response is to decrease output.

Economic mechanism: Output expansion by rival reduces residual demand; optimal response is to contract.

Bertrand Model with Differentiated Products

Demand functions:

\[Q_1 = \tilde{a} - \tilde{b}p_1 + \tilde{d}p_2\] \[Q_2 = \tilde{a} + \tilde{d}p_1 - \tilde{b}p_2\]

Transformed parameters: - \(\tilde{a} = \frac{a}{b+d}\) - \(\tilde{b} = \frac{b}{b^2 - d^2}\) - \(\tilde{d} = \frac{d}{b^2 - d^2}\)

Bertrand Best Response with Substitutes

For substitute goods (\(d > 0\)):

\[BR_i(p_j) = \frac{\tilde{a} + \tilde{d}p_j + \tilde{b}c_i}{2\tilde{b}}\]

Property: Upward-sloping best response.

Strategic Complements: Interpretation

Prices are strategic complements:

If competitor raises price, my optimal response is to raise price.

Economic mechanism: Price increase by rival softens competition; optimal response is to maintain price alignment.

Equilibrium Prices: Cournot vs. Bertrand

Cournot equilibrium (\(c_i = 0\)):

\[p_i^C = \frac{ab}{2b + d}\]

Bertrand equilibrium (\(c_i = 0\)):

\[p_i^B = \frac{a(b-d)}{2b - d}\]

Price Comparison: Cournot vs. Bertrand

Price difference:

\[p_i^C - p_i^B = \frac{ad^2}{4b^2 - d^2} > 0\]

Result: Bertrand prices are strictly lower than Cournot prices.

Competition Intensity: Interpretation

Key Finding

Price competition (Bertrand) leads to lower prices and larger quantities than quantity competition (Cournot).

The strategic variable selection determines equilibrium price, output, and total welfare.

Oligopoly Coordination

Tacit Collusion Problem

Can competing firms coordinate pricing without explicit contracts?

Obstacles: - Individual profit motives conflict with group interest - No legal mechanism to enforce agreements - Constant temptation to cheat on agreements

Coordination Failure in Oligopoly

Result: The non-cooperative (Nash) equilibrium is Pareto-dominated by coordinated outcomes.

Implication: Firms would jointly profit from coordination but cannot achieve it without commitment devices.

Facilitating Devices: Definition

Facilitating devices are contractual mechanisms that:

  • Increase incentives to cooperate
  • Decrease incentives to cheat

Legal examples: - Most-Favoured-Nation (MFN) clauses - Most-Favoured-Customer (MFC) clauses

Facilitating Devices: Competition Policy

Policy Scrutiny

These devices alter payoff structures in ways that enable tacit collusion; competition authorities scrutinize them accordingly.

The Prisoner’s Dilemma: Payoff Matrix

Two firms, one period:

High Price Low Price
High Price 100, 100 -10, 140
Low Price 140, -10 70, 70

Prisoner’s Dilemma: Nash Equilibrium

Nash equilibrium: \((p_L, p_L)\) with payoffs 70, 70

Cooperative outcome: \((p_H, p_H)\) with payoffs 100, 100

Problem: Cooperation is not a Nash equilibrium without commitment or repeated-game structure.

Incentive Incompatibility: Sequential Logic

Starting from \((p_L, p_L)\), why cannot both firms coordinate on \((p_H, p_H)\)?

  1. First-mover disadvantage: Leader gets payoff \(-10\) while follower earns \(140\)
  2. Incentive to delay: Follower waits to capture the \(140\)
  3. Leader’s temptation to revert: Seeing no response, leader drops back to \(p_L\)

Coordination Failure: Equilibrium Characterization

Unique Nash Equilibrium

Unilateral deviation is profitable from any proposed high-price agreement; hence the prisoner’s dilemma equilibrium \((p_L, p_L)\) is the unique Nash equilibrium of the simultaneous-move game.

Adjusted Payoff Matrix: Coordination Solution

Reduce follower’s transitional gain:

High Price Low Price
High Price 100, 100 -10, 90
Low Price 90, -10 70, 70

Result: \((p_H, p_H)\) is now a Nash equilibrium.

Adjusted Equilibrium: Interpretation

If firm moves to \(p_H\): - Earns \(-10\) in the transition - Given opponent is at \(p_H\), best response is also \(p_H\), earning \(100\)

Both firms converge to \((p_H, p_H)\) with payoffs 100, 100 (symmetric equilibrium).

Most-Favoured-Nation (MFN) Clause

Definition:

A seller commits to: - Charge a specified minimum price - Refund buyers if a lower price is offered later - Pay penalties equivalent to price differences

MFN Effect on Deviation Incentives

Effect on deviation cost:

Action Without MFN With MFN
Price cut benefit Capture all new buyers Lose many to refunds
Price cut cost Minimal Refund obligations + penalties

Result: MFN raises the cost of deviation from high price.

MFN Self-Enforcement Condition

For MFN to work, it must be self-enforcing:

\[\text{Gross profit from deviation} - \text{Refunds} < \text{Profit under MFN}\]

Implication: Deviation becomes unprofitable.

MFN Welfare Effect

Free Rider Effect in Reverse (Salop 1986)

Individual buyers value MFN protection, but collectively it harms them by preventing sellers from offering discounts to future customers.

Numerical Example: Bertrand Duopoly Setup

Model:

Demand: \[Q_1 = 3 - 2p_1 + p_2\] \[Q_2 = 3 + p_1 - 2p_2\]

Costs: \(c_1 = c_2 = 1\)

Baseline Bertrand Equilibrium

Best response functions:

\[BR_i(p_j) = \frac{5 + p_j}{4}\]

Equilibrium:

\[p_1^B = p_2^B = \frac{5}{3} \approx 1.667\] \[\pi_i^B = \frac{8}{9} \approx 0.889\]

MFN Adoption: Firm 1’s Commitment

Firm 1 commits to price:

\[p_1^* = \frac{12}{7} \approx 1.714\]

Firm 2’s best response:

\[p_2^* = BR_2(p_1^*) = \frac{47}{28} \approx 1.678\]

MFN Outcome: Firm 1’s Profit

Outcomes:

\[Q_1^* = \frac{5}{4}\] \[\pi_1^* = \frac{25}{28} \approx 0.893 > 0.889\]

Note

Firm 1’s profits rise to \(\approx 0.893\) from the Bertrand equilibrium level of \(0.889\) by committing to a higher price via the MFN clause.

Deviation from MFN: Gross Profit

Firm 1 considers deviating to:

\[\tilde{p}_1 = BR_1(p_2^*) = \frac{187}{112} \approx 1.669\]

Gross profit from deviation:

\[\tilde{Q}_1 = \frac{75}{56}\] \[\tilde{\pi}_1 = \frac{5625}{6272} \approx 0.897\]

Deviation from MFN: Refund Obligation

MFN requires refunds:

\[\text{Refunds} = Q_1^* (p_1^* - \tilde{p}_1) = \frac{25}{448} \approx 0.056\]

Net profit after refunds:

\[\tilde{\pi}_1 - \text{Refunds} = \frac{5275}{6272} \approx 0.841\]

MFN Self-Enforcement: Verification

No Profitable Deviation

Net profit from deviation (\(0.841\)) is strictly less than profit under MFN commitment (\(0.893\)).

The MFN clause is thus self-enforcing: no firm has incentive to deviate unilaterally.

Summary: Tacit Collusion

Key takeaways:

  1. Coordination problem: Joint profit maximization yields higher payoffs than Nash equilibrium
  2. Single-period dilemma: The Nash equilibrium is Pareto-inefficient
  3. Facilitating devices: MFN clauses alter the payoff structure to align individual and collective incentives
  4. Self-enforcement: Properly structured MFN clauses render deviation less profitable than compliance
