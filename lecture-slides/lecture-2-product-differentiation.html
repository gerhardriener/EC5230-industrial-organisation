Lecture 2 – Product Differentiation

Gerhard Riener

Product differentiation: overview

Object of analysis

Horizontal differentiation: products differ in characteristics; consumers incur mismatch costs.

  • Horizontal v vertical differentiation
    • What determines demand when prices are equal?
    • How does variety interact with quantity?
  • Focus today: horizontal product differentiation
    • Linear Hotelling model (two firms)
    • Salop’s circular market (free entry)

Linear Hotelling model: setting

  • Market structure
    • Duopoly; zero marginal cost
    • Linear market of length \(L\); consumers uniformly distributed
    • Each consumer buys either 1 unit or nothing
  • Preference parameters
    • Firm 1 located at distance \(a\) from left endpoint (0)
    • Firm 2 located at distance \(b\) from right endpoint (\(L\))
    • Linear transport cost: \(C(d)=cd\) where \(d\) is distance and \(c>0\)
  • Objects
    • Choice variables: \(p_1, p_2\)
    • Parameters: \(L, a, b, c\)
    • Equilibrium outcomes: \(x, y, q_1, q_2\)

Linear Hotelling: pricing with fixed locations (Firm 1)

  • Demand derivation
    • Marginal consumer indifferent: \(p_1 + cx = p_2 + cy\)
    • Market length: \(L = a + b + x + y\)
    • Firm 1 demand: \(q_1 = a + x = \frac{p_2 - p_1 + c(L + a - b)}{2c}\)
  • Profit and best response
    • \(\pi_1 = p_1 q_1\)
    • \(BR_1(p_2)=\frac{p_2}{2}+\frac{c}{2}(L+a-b)\)

Interpretation (best response)

  • \(BR_1\) is increasing in \(p_2\) (prices are strategic complements here)
  • Larger \(c\) softens competition, shifting best responses upward

Linear Hotelling: pricing with fixed locations (Firm 2)

  • Demand derivation
    • Marginal consumer indifferent: \(p_1 + cx = p_2 + cy\)
    • Market length: \(L = a + b + x + y\)
    • Firm 2 demand: \(q_2 = b + y = \frac{p_1 - p_2 + c(L + b - a)}{2c}\)
  • Profit and best response
    • \(\pi_2 = p_2 q_2\)
    • \(BR_2(p_1)=\frac{p_1}{2}+\frac{c}{2}(L+b-a)\)

Interpretation (symmetry)

  • If \(a=b\), best responses are symmetric and yield \(p_1^*=p_2^*\)

Linear Hotelling model: Nash equilibrium in prices

  • Best responses
    • \(BR_1(p_2) = \frac{p_2}{2} + \frac{c}{2}(L + a - b)\)
    • \(BR_2(p_1) = \frac{p_1}{2} + \frac{c}{2}(L + b - a)\)
  • Nash equilibrium prices and profits
    • \(p_1^* = c\left(L + \frac{a-b}{3}\right)\); \(p_2^* = c\left(L + \frac{b-a}{3}\right)\)
    • \(\pi_1^* = \frac{c}{18}(3L + a - b)^2\); \(\pi_2^* = \frac{c}{18}(3L + b - a)^2\)

Key comparative static

Higher mismatch cost \(c\) lowers substitutability and raises equilibrium markups and profits.

Linear Hotelling model: incentive to move (co-location)

  • If firms locate at the same point
    • Then \(a + b = L\) and the marginal consumer collapses to the location point: \(x=y=0\)
    • Products become homogeneous \(\Rightarrow\) Bertrand undercutting
    • Unique equilibrium: \(p_1^B=p_2^B=0\) and \(\pi_1^B=\pi_2^B=0\)

Co-location and price competition

Co-location removes captive consumers; any \(p>0\) can be profitably undercut.

Linear Hotelling model: incentive to move (separated)

  • Marginal incentive to increase own location parameter
    • \(\frac{\partial \pi_1^*}{\partial a} = \frac{c}{9}(3L + a - b) > 0\)
    • \(\frac{\partial \pi_2^*}{\partial b} = \frac{c}{9}(3L + b - a) > 0\)
  • Interpretation (pricing subgame only)
    • Increasing \(a\) (resp. \(b\)) moves Firm 1 (resp. Firm 2) inward (toward the centre)
    • These are comparative statics of equilibrium profits in the pricing subgame, holding the rival location fixed

Linear Hotelling model: location equilibrium (linear v quadratic transport)

  • Result (standard)
    • With linear transport costs (\(C(d)=cd\)), the full two-stage location game may fail to have a pure-strategy equilibrium in locations
  • Resolution
    • d’Aspremont, Gabszewicz & Thisse (1979): with quadratic transport costs (\(C(d)=td^2\)), a pure-strategy location equilibrium exists (maximum differentiation in their baseline)

Why convexity matters

More convex mismatch costs make small location changes less effective at stealing marginal consumers through pricing.

Linear Hotelling model: profit function

Profit function of Firm 1 (d’Aspremont, Gabszewicz & Thisse, 1979):

\[ \pi_1(p_1, p_2) = \begin{cases} \frac{p_1 p_2}{2c} - \frac{p_1^2}{2c} + \frac{(L+a-b)p_1}{2} & \text{if } |p_1 - p_2| \le c(L - a - b) \\[0.5em] Lp_1 & \text{if } p_1 < p_2 - c(L - a - b) \\[0.5em] 0 & \text{if } p_1 > p_2 + c(L - a - b) \end{cases} \]

  • Region 1 (interior): both firms serve positive mass of consumers; demand is linear in \(p_1\)

  • Region 2 (market capture): Firm 1 undercuts enough to serve the whole line segment

  • Region 3 (no demand): Firm 1 sets too high a price; demand is zero

  • Interpretation

    • Kinks occur at the thresholds where a marginal consumer hits an endpoint of the market
    • Piecewise structure clarifies why global profit maximisation can differ from the interior FOC

Salop’s circular market: setting

  • Market structure
    • Two sectors: one with homogeneous outside good; one with differentiated goods
    • Circular market circumference normalised to 1; consumers uniformly distributed
    • \(n\) firms symmetrically located; distance between adjacent firms is \(\frac{1}{n}\)
    • Each consumer buys either one unit (from some firm) or the outside good
  • Objects
    • Choice variable: \(p_i\)
    • Parameters: \(n, c, u, u^*\)
    • Outcomes: market boundary/location cutoffs, quantity per firm

Salop’s circular market: consumer utility

Participation constraint

Let \(v=u-u^*\). A consumer participates in the differentiated sector iff \(v \ge p_j + cd_j\).

  • Preference heterogeneity
    • Each consumer \(i\) has ideal variety \(\ell_i^*\) (location on the circle)
    • Linear transport (mismatch) cost: \(cd\) where \(d\) is arc distance
  • Utility from purchase
    • Buy outside good: \(U^O = u^*\)
    • Buy from firm \(j\): \(U_j^D = u - c d_j - p_j\)
  • Participation condition
    • Consumer buys from firm \(j\) iff \(U_j^D \ge U^O\)
    • Let \(v = u - u^*\), so participation is \(v \ge p_j + c d_j\)
  • Interpretation
    • \(v\) is the net gross surplus from consuming a perfectly matched differentiated variety rather than the outside good
    • Transport costs convert “distance in product space” into a monetary disutility

Salop’s circular market: monopoly region (no competition)

  • Monopoly scenario

    • Firm’s market is truncated by the outside option (participation), not by neighbouring firms

  • Maximum distance: \(d_m = \frac{v - p}{c}\)

  • Monopoly demand: \(q^M = 2 d_m = 2\left(\frac{v-p}{c}\right)\)

  • Inverse demand: \(p = v - \frac{c}{2} q^M\)

  • Interpretation

    • Demand slope is \(-\frac{c}{2}\) because increasing quantity expands the served interval on both sides of the firm
    • Participation binds at the marginal consumer: \(v = p + cd_m\)

Salop’s circular market: competitive region (with neighbors)

  • Competitive scenario
    • Market boundary determined by indifference between this firm and neighbour(s)
  • Indifference condition
    • Main firm: \(v - cx - p\)
    • Neighbour: \(v - c\left(\frac{1}{n} - x\right) - \tilde{p}\)
  • Solve for boundary
    • \(cx - p = c\left(\frac{1}{n} - x\right) - \tilde{p}\)
    • \(x = \frac{\frac{c}{n} - p + \tilde{p}}{2c}\)
  • Interpretation
    • The boundary \(x\) depends on both own price \(p\) and neighbour price \(\tilde p\)

Salop’s circular market: competitive demand and inverse demand

  • Competitive demand: \(q^C = 2x = \frac{\frac{c}{n} - p + \tilde{p}}{c}\)

  • Inverse demand: \(p = \frac{c}{n} + \tilde{p} - c q^C\)

Slope comparison

Competitive-region inverse demand is steeper than monopoly-region inverse demand: \(-c\) vs \(-\frac{c}{2}\).

Salop’s circular market: kinked demand

  • Firm’s choice between regimes
    • Monopoly region: constrained by participation (outside option)
    • Competitive region: constrained by neighbours (local oligopoly)
  • Interpretation
    • As parameters change (e.g. \(v, c, n\)), the relevant constraint switches, generating a kink in the inverse demand
    • This matters for pricing incentives because marginal revenue changes discretely at the regime boundary

Salop’s circular market: kinked demand (location of kink)

  • Two candidate constraints
    • Participation binds when the marginal consumer is indifferent between firm \(i\) and the outside good: \(v = p + cd\)
    • Neighbour competition binds when the marginal consumer is indifferent between firm \(i\) and neighbour: \(v - cx - p = v - c(\frac{1}{n}-x) - \tilde p\)
  • Kink
    • The relevant constraint switches at the quantity where the two implied boundaries coincide

Salop’s circular market: free-entry equilibrium (supply side)

  • Cost structure
    • \(C(q)=F + mq\) with fixed cost \(F\) and constant marginal cost \(m\)
    • Average cost: \(AC(q)=m+\frac{F}{q}\) is decreasing in \(q\)
  • Firm choice in competitive region
    • Demand: \(p = \frac{c}{n} + \tilde{p} - cq\)
    • Best response: \(MR = MC\) where \(MR = \frac{c}{n} + \tilde{p} - 2cq\)
  • Assumption: equilibrium is in competitive region for all firms; by symmetry \(\tilde{p} = p\)

Salop’s circular market: free-entry equilibrium (zero-profit condition)

  • Symmetric competitive equilibrium
    • In symmetry: \(p=\tilde{p}\) and \(q^C=\frac{1}{n}\)
  • Zero profit condition (free entry)
    • \(p^C = AC = m + \frac{F}{q^C}\)
    • Substitute \(q^C = \frac{1}{n}\): \(p^C = m + nF\)
  • Interpretation
    • Free entry drives firms to earn zero economic profit
    • Entry cost \(F\) creates a trade-off: more firms means lower profit per firm

Salop’s circular market: free-entry equilibrium (first-order condition)

  • First-order condition (optimal quantity)
    • \(MR = MC\) ⇔ \(p^C + q^C \frac{dp^C}{dq^C} = m\)
    • Slope in competitive region: \(\frac{dp}{dq} = -c\)
    • Therefore: \(p^C - cq^C = m\) ⇔ \(p^C = m + \frac{c}{n}\)
  • Solve for equilibrium
    • Equate zero-profit and FOC: \(m + nF = m + \frac{c}{n}\)
    • \(n^C = \sqrt{\frac{c}{F}}\); \(p^C = m + \sqrt{cF}\)
  • Interpretation
    • \(n^C\) increases with mismatch intensity \(c\) (more differentiation supports more firms)
    • \(n^C\) decreases with fixed cost \(F\) (entry becomes less profitable)

Salop’s circular market: welfare analysis (consumer cost)

  • Welfare object
    • Consumers’ average distance to nearest firm: \(\bar d = \frac{1}{4n}\) (half the arc between adjacent firms)
    • Resource cost of variety: \(nF\)
  • Per-consumer utilitarian welfare
    • With quasilinear utility, prices are transfers
    • \(W(n) = v - \frac{c}{4n} - nF\), where \(v = u - u^*\)
  • Interpretation
    • Welfare depends on mismatch costs (↓ in \(n\)) and fixed entry costs (↑ in \(n\))
    • Social planner internalizes both effects; firms do not

Salop’s circular market: welfare analysis (social optimum)

  • Social planner’s problem: choose \(n\) to maximize \(W(n)\)
    • \(\frac{dW}{dn} = \frac{c}{4n^2} - F = 0\)
    • \(n^S = \frac{1}{2}\sqrt{\frac{c}{F}} = \frac{1}{2} n^C\)
  • Equilibrium v optimum
    • Free-entry equilibrium: \(n^C = \sqrt{\frac{c}{F}}\)
    • Social optimum: \(n^S = \frac{1}{2}n^C\)

Excess entry

In this benchmark, free entry implies \(n^C = 2n^S\).

Summary & references

Summary

  • Horizontal differentiation relaxes price competition via consumer mismatch costs
  • Hotelling (fixed locations): equilibrium prices increase in transport cost \(c\)
  • Location incentives under linear transport can push toward minimum differentiation; quadratic costs restore a pure-strategy location equilibrium
  • Salop (circle): free entry implies \(n^C=\sqrt{\frac{c}{F}}\) and excessive entry relative to the social optimum \(n^S=\frac{1}{2}n^C\)

References
