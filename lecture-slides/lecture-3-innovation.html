Lecture 3 - Innovation

Gerhard Riener

Innovation and Market Structure

Innovation: types and stages

Why study innovation?

  • Innovation is a stochastic process
  • Probability of success depends on resources allocated

Types:

  • Process innovation: shifts cost function (typically lowers marginal cost)
  • Product innovation: shifts demand function

Stages:

  • Research → Development → Adoption

Innovation and competition: two-way relation

Two-way relationship:

  • How market structure affects incentives to innovate
  • How innovation affects market structure

Key question:

  • Compare maximum willingness to pay (WTP) for innovation across market structures:
    1. Monopoly (before and after innovation)
    2. Perfect competition (before; monopoly rights after)
    3. Social planner

Interpretation:

  • Firm WTP: maximum lump-sum payment that leaves profit unchanged, i.e. \(\text{WTP} = \Delta \pi\)
  • Planner WTP: maximum lump-sum payment that leaves welfare unchanged, i.e. \(\text{WTP} = \Delta W\)

Market Structure and Innovation Incentives

Example: linear demand and process innovation

Market demand (inverse):

\[ P(Q) = A - Q \]

Process innovation:

  • Marginal cost falls from \(c_0\) to \(c_1\) where \(c_1 < c_0 < A\)

Variables:

  • Parameters: \(A, c_0, c_1\)
  • Choice variables: \(Q\) (monopoly / planner), and later \(p\) in price competition
  • Outcomes: \(Q^m(c)\), \(P^m(c)\), \(\pi^m(c)\)

Monopoly (before and after innovation): problem and FOC

Monopoly problem:

\[ \max_Q \ (A - Q - c)Q \]

First-order condition:

\[ A - 2Q - c = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad Q^m(c) = \frac{A-c}{2} \]

Interpretation:

  • \(Q\) is the choice variable; \(A\) and \(c\) are parameters
  • Lower \(c\) increases the monopoly’s optimal output

Monopoly (before and after innovation): outcomes and WTP

Equilibrium outcomes:

  • Price: \(P^m(c) = \frac{A+c}{2}\)
  • Profit: \(\pi^m(c) = \frac{(A-c)^2}{4}\)

Max WTP for innovation:

\[ \Delta \pi^m = \pi^m(c_1) - \pi^m(c_0) = \frac{(A-c_1)^2 - (A-c_0)^2}{4} \]

Interpretation:

  • \(\Delta \pi^m\) is the monopoly’s value of exclusive access to the lower cost \(c_1\)

Perfect competition (before; monopoly rights after)

Before innovation (all firms at MC \(c_0\)):

  • Competitive price: \(P^{pc}_0 = c_0\)
  • Firm profit: \(\pi = 0\)

After innovation (innovator has exclusive use):

  • If innovation is drastic: innovator behaves as monopolist at cost \(c_1\)
  • If non-drastic: innovator constrained by competitive fringe at \(c_0\)

Key insight:

  • Competitive firm can be willing to pay more than monopolist
  • Creates profit rather than replacing existing profit

Interpretation:

  • Under perfect competition pre-innovation, rents are (approximately) zero, so innovation primarily creates rents for the innovator
  • Under monopoly pre-innovation, some rents already exist, so innovation partly replaces them

Size of process innovation: drastic v non-drastic

Definitions:

  • Drastic (major) innovation: innovator behaves as unconstrained monopolist
  • Non-drastic (minor) innovation: innovator has cost advantage but remains constrained by rivals

Criterion:

Innovation is drastic if monopoly price at cost \(c_1\) falls below rivals’ cost \(c_0\):

\[ P^m(c_1) < c_0 \quad \Leftrightarrow \quad \frac{A+c_1}{2} < c_0 \quad \Leftrightarrow \quad A + c_1 < 2c_0 \]

Interpretation:

  • If \(P^m(c_1) \ge c_0\), the fringe constrains price (limit pricing), so monopoly pricing at cost \(c_1\) is not feasible
  • “Drastic” is a statement about whether rivals are binding constraints on the innovator’s pricing

Replacement effect (Arrow, 1972)

Key result:

  • Monopolist has positive pre-innovation profits → innovation partly replaces existing profits
  • Competitive firm earns (near) zero pre-innovation profits → innovation creates new profits

Implication:

  • Competitive firm may be willing to pay more than monopolist for same innovation
  • Pre-existing market power can reduce innovation incentives (ceteris paribus)

Caveat:

  • With entry threat, incumbents may value innovation for pre-emption (later section)

Social planner: efficient output

Efficient output for demand \(P(Q)=A-Q\) and marginal cost \(c\):

\[ P(Q) = c \quad \Rightarrow \quad Q^{sp}(c) = A - c \]

Interpretation:

  • Efficiency sets price equal to marginal cost (allocative efficiency)
  • Lower \(c\) increases the efficient quantity one-for-one

Social planner: welfare and WTP

Total surplus (welfare):

\[ W(c) = \int_0^{A-c} (A - Q - c) \, dQ = \frac{(A-c)^2}{2} \]

Max WTP for innovation (social planner):

\[ \Delta W = W(c_1) - W(c_0) = \frac{(A-c_1)^2 - (A-c_0)^2}{2} \]

Interpretation:

  • The planner values innovation more than a monopolist because it internalises the output expansion effect

Oligopoly and Innovation

Incentives to innovate in oligopoly

Earlier ranking (under specific assumptions):

  • Max WTP: Social planner > Competitive firm > Monopoly

But in oligopoly:

  • Incentives need not be monotone in competition intensity
  • Key drivers:
    • Number of firms (\(n\))
    • Product differentiation
    • Type of competition (price vs quantity)

Interpretation:

  • Innovation changes relative costs or demands; its payoff depends on how strongly rivals’ actions respond (strategic interaction)

Impact of number of firms (Cournot intuition)

Linear Cournot with \(n\) firms:

  • Innovation incentives can follow inverse-U as \(n\) increases

Two opposing effects as \(n \uparrow\):

  • Competition effect: profits for all firms fall (discourages innovation)
  • Competitive advantage effect: cost advantage becomes more valuable when there are more inefficient rivals (encourages innovation)

Interpretation:

  • “More rivals” reduces the baseline profit level, but increases the value of being the low-cost firm relative to the rest

Monopoly threatened by entry: setup

Incumbent monopoly faces potential entrant:

  • Both can acquire innovation reducing MC from \(c_0\) to \(c_1 < c_0\)
  • Entrant can enter profitably only if it gets innovation

Payoffs:

  • If incumbent gets innovation: entrant stays out
    • Incumbent: \(\pi^m(c_1)\); Entrant: \(0\)
  • If entrant gets innovation: entrant enters
    • Incumbent: \(\pi^d(c_0, c_1)\); Entrant: \(\pi^d(c_1, c_0)\)

Interpretation:

  • Innovation has a strategic value by changing market structure (monopoly vs duopoly)

Efficiency effect (Gilbert and Newbery, 1982): values

Per-period value of innovation:

\[ V_I = \pi^m(c_1) - \pi^d(c_0, c_1) \]

\[ V_E = \pi^d(c_1, c_0) \]

Interpretation:

  • Incumbent value \(V_I\) measures the loss avoided by preventing entry (monopoly preserved)
  • Entrant value \(V_E\) is the profit created by entering with the cost advantage

Efficiency effect (Gilbert and Newbery, 1982): condition

Incumbent has higher incentives if:

\[ V_I > V_E \quad \Leftrightarrow \quad \pi^m(c_1) > \pi^d(c_1, c_0) + \pi^d(c_0, c_1) \]

Holds typically when products are close substitutes

Interpretation:

  • The incumbent internalises the effect of innovation on market structure (monopoly vs duopoly)

Seminal Contributions

Dasgupta and Stiglitz (1980): setup

Question: Does more firms imply more innovation?

Primitives:

  • \(U(Q)\) = gross social benefit
  • \(c = c(x)\) = constant marginal cost depending on R&D expenditure \(x\)
  • Assumption: \(c'(x) < 0\) (R&D reduces cost)

Social optimum:

\[ \max_{x,Q} \ V(x,Q) = U(Q) - c(x)Q - x \]

First-order condition in \(x\):

\[ -c'(x)Q = 1 \]

Interpretation:

  • Optimal R&D increases with scale \(Q\): a larger market makes cost reduction more valuable

Dasgupta and Stiglitz (1980): firm problem

Firm \(i\)’s problem (symmetric firms):

\[ \max_{x_i, q_i} \ \pi(x_i, q_i) = [P(q_i + q_{-i}) - c(x_i)]q_i - x_i \]

Symmetric equilibrium:

  • \(q_1^* = \cdots = q_N^* = Q^*/N\) and \(x_1^* = \cdots = x_N^* = x^*\)

First-order condition in \(q_i\):

\[ P(Q^*)\left(1 - \frac{\varepsilon}{N}\right) = c(x^*) \]

where \(\varepsilon = -\frac{\partial Q}{\partial P}\frac{P}{Q}\) is demand elasticity

Interpretation:

  • The term \(\left(1-\frac{\varepsilon}{N}\right)\) captures market power via the perceived demand elasticity under Cournot

Dasgupta and Stiglitz (1980): free entry (zero profit)

Zero profit condition (free entry):

\[ \pi = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad (P - c)\frac{Q^*}{N^*} - x^* = 0 \]

Rearrange:

\[ P - c = N^* \frac{x^*}{Q^*} \]

Dasgupta and Stiglitz (1980): free entry (implication)

Key implication: Concentration and research intensity are jointly determined (no simple causal link)

Interpretation:

  • Entry expands \(N\) until operating surplus covers R&D outlays \(x^*\)

Dasgupta and Stiglitz (1980): equilibrium concentration

Define innovative potential:

\[ \alpha = -\frac{dc(x)}{dx}\frac{x}{c} \]

Equilibrium concentration satisfies:

\[ \frac{1}{N^*} = \frac{1}{\varepsilon} \cdot \frac{\alpha}{1+\alpha} \]

Interpretation:

  • Higher \(\alpha\) (greater innovative potential) → higher concentration
  • More elastic demand → lower concentration

Dasgupta (1986): additional results (part 1)

With \(P(Q) = \sigma Q^{-\varepsilon}\) and \(c(x) = \beta x^{-\alpha}\):

Dynamic vs static efficiency trade-off:

  • \(x^*(N+1) < x^*(N)\) (more firms → less R&D per firm)
  • \(Q^*(N+1) > Q^*(N)\) (more firms → higher output)

Research intensity:

  • Often maximized at moderate concentration (Scherer-style inverse-U)

Interpretation:

  • Market structure affects both static outcomes (\(Q\)) and dynamic investment incentives (\(x\))

Dasgupta (1986): additional results (part 2)

Greater innovative opportunities associated with higher concentration:

\[ \frac{\partial (1/N^*)}{\partial \alpha} > 0 \]

Demand growth stimulates R&D:

\[ \frac{\partial x^*}{\partial \sigma} > 0 \]

Interpretation:

  • Larger markets (higher \(\sigma\)) increase the return to cost reduction
  • Stronger innovative opportunities (higher \(\alpha\)) can support higher concentration in equilibrium

Summary

  • Innovation value depends on the objective: \(\Delta \pi\) (firm) versus \(\Delta W\) (planner)
  • Replacement effect: pre-innovation rents reduce the monopolist’s incremental gain from innovation
  • With entry threat, innovation has strategic value via market structure (monopoly vs duopoly)
  • In oligopoly, incentives reflect competing forces (competition effect vs advantage effect)
